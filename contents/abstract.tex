\begin{abstract}

Video-LLMs face a fundamental tension in long-video reasoning: static, sparse frame sampling either dilutes evidence across task-irrelevant segments or misses fine-grained temporal semantics altogether. We formulate \underline{\textbf{T}}emporal \underline{\textbf{V}}isual \underline{\textbf{S}}creening (TVS) as a \textit{structurally-constrained compression problem} grounded in a generative view of VideoQA: given the Markov chain $A \to (V,Q) \to (v,q)$, TVS seeks the minimal $A$-sufficient statistic of the (video, query) pair under temporal-continuity constraints---an endomorphic transformation $\mathcal{F}_{TVS}:(V,Q) \to (v,q)$ instantiating the Information Bottleneck principle directly in the original bimodal task space. Its two objectives---video-side compression and query-side reasoning minimization---collapse onto a single operating point via constraint re-allocation: each reasoning step removable from $q$ is structurally pre-satisfied by trimming $V$, so the two descend in lockstep. TVS thereby conserves the task's constraint budget across modalities rather than relaxing the query or retrieving evidence. As a modular front-end, TVS plugs into \textbf{both} Video Instruction Tuning and Video Question Answering pipelines. We release the first dedicated benchmark and propose ReSimplifyIt, a baseline surpassing prior methods by 0.40 F-1 with competitive query rewriting. Integrating TVS yields relative gains of 7.33\% in training and 33.7\% in inference for video-language understanding.\footnote{Our code is available \href{https://github.com/LordUky/Temporal-Visual-Screening}{here}.}

\end{abstract}
